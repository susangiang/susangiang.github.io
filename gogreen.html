Go Green

Making conscious decisions and better habits for waste disposal

role

designer, sept - dec 2016

I was the lead designer in a team of 7 (one designer, five developers, and a product manager) throughout all phases of the design process. I mainly pushed pixels and led the creative direction of the product using user research findings.

the problem

reducing consumer waste

As of 2012, there are 251 million tonnes of waste in the United States, where 35 million tonnes of it are food waste and are often reusable. Current methods of disposal include incineration or transportation to landfills, which is inefficient, toxic, space consuming, and creates pollutant emissions. We wanted to create a solution that would help reduce consumer waste in the long run in hopes of creating better, informed waste disposal decisions and habits.

the goal

educating the younger generation to make conscious decisions

To raise environmental awareness and consciousness, children should be better educated by making salient the amount of waste they are producing, which could affect their future purchasing decisions and influence them to adopt proper waste disposal habits as they grow older.

workflow

I drew the storyboard to outline the end-to-end experience of using the product, from discovering the product and purchasing the physical component, to setting up the accompanied mobile app and successfully using its features to the user’s benefits.

Initial storyboarding

the prototype

The first prototype was a mobile app with a 5-page interface that leverages weight sensing technology and gamification to incentivize children to reduce their total waste output. It does this by syncing with a weight sensor installed at the bottom of a garbage can and awarding more points to the user when the garbage can weight is under a certain threshold during garbage collection day. The child can then use these points to purchase trees that will be planted in their local community on their behalf, which serves as an incentive for the user to throw away less waste.

Applying design heuristics for mobile application design, I made the following hi-fidelity mockups on Adobe Photoshop to be used for user testing.

user testing

putting our prototype to the test

The first prototype was tested with a total of five users, two of which were users in the target age range and three of which have younger siblings within the target range. The prototype was tested on an iPad and users were instructed to go through the experience outlined in the storyboard I had previously created. They were told that their ultimate goal is to plant a tree successfully, without telling them how they could achieve it on the app.

The tasks to be performed were the following:

  • Setting up the actual sensor(unboxing the garbage can and attaching the weight sensor)
  • Log onto mobile app
  • Check app after the waste in the garbage can has been collected by a garbage truck (users are told to pretend that some time has passed since setting up in Task 1)
  • Achieve their ultimate goal of planting a tree successfully

results

the product wasn’t as intuitive and helpful as we hoped it would be

The first prototype had an emphasis on presenting a data-based analysis for the user’s garbage disposal. Since we aimed for our prototype to reduce waste through education and the cultivation of good habits, we made the initial assumption that providing trends and data through our product would be useful as users would be able to reflect upon it and adjust their garbage disposal habits accordingly. We soon realized during user testing that this prototype did not work well for our target audience of younger children.

The following feedback was given by most of the users during user testing:

“How does the app work?”

All of the users had a difficult time onboarding and understanding the value of the app–there was not enough guidance to allow them to reap the app’s benefits to its fullest

“How is this a game?”

It is difficult to tell whether the app is a game or not, and how it is supposed to reduce waste. The test subjects that were in the targeted age range did not understand the connection between earning points in the game and using these points to plant a tree in real life; it did not appeal to them whatsoever.

“Is this really an app for children?”

Test subjects with younger siblings thought that the sophisticated graphs and statistics on the app would not be understood by their siblings. They also thought that there was too much text and not enough child-friendly visual elements to keep them engaged and motivated.

iterating

back to the drawing board

Based on the feedback received from user testing, we made the following design decisions:

“How does the app work?”

It was very important to us that the user understands the value of the app and how they can take advantage of everything that it has to offer. To make onboarding a smoother process and provide the user with more context on the app itself, we decided to include a mandatory tutorial modal appear upon first log-in; the tutorial consists of several screens that outlines the purpose of each page of the app and how the user can interact with it. We have also decided to include a Help modal that the user can access at the bottom of every page for further assistance in the future.

“How is this a game?”

As great as planting trees on behalf of the user in the real world sounds, it wasn’t motivating enough for our target users; we found that children preferred virtual games as opposed to some reward in real life. As a result, we replaced the ‘Plant My Tree’ screen with ‘My Garden’, a feature that allows users to plant seeds and grow them into different trees in a virtual interface. The user can redeem the points that they earn in the app to buy seeds and use the toolbox in the game to cultivate and water their seeds into trees.

To further gamify the app, we added a ‘Community’ screen, which is essentially a leaderboard that allows users to track their score and other metrics against friends that they have added to their network. This introduces a social aspect into the app, where users can interact with their friends and are also strongly incentivized by competition amongst their peers.

“Is this really an app for children?”

The graphs on the Dashboard were difficult for children to understand. To eliminate the use of confusing numbers and complicated graphs, I redesigned the screen to be as simple as possible by using a garbage bin icon to represent the user’s actual garbage bin at home. As more waste is added to the bin, the weight sensor would sense the increase in weight, which results in an increase to the size of the garbage bin on the app and even changes colours to indicate warning levels once a certain weight threshold has been reached.

The scoring system was also revamped; previously, the user was only awarded points once a week (whenever the garbage bin was emptied during weekly waste collection). We found that allocating points this way relied heavily on the user’s patience and was simply too long of a wait for the user to want to continue using the app on a daily basis. Therefore, the new scoring system continuously rewards the user with a certain number of points if they can keep the weight of the waste below a specific threshold. Once that threshold is reached, the user would continue to earn points, but they would receive a lower amount. This is because we believed that scoring should provide instant and positive feedback to the user; children did not like to wait and losing points would strongly discourage them from using the app again.

This solution allows the user to understand the correlation between an increase in waste weight and the number of points they would earn, thus motivating them to reduce the amount of waste they dispose of in the long run.

To make the UI appear more child-friendly, I increased the use of iconography and reduced text usage within the screens. The main navigation was also moved to the bottom of the screen to comply with iOS conventions and accommodate for the smaller hand sizes of children.

Revising the workflow

the final solution

Based on the results of feedback received from user testing, the final solution was a 4-page interface mobile app with a new scoring scheme that awards additional points to the user for keeping the garbage can at a low weight for as long as possible, while awarding less points to the user if the weight increases. The gamification aspect was also changed to allow the child to use these points to purchase seeds and tools for planting trees in their virtual garden, which serves as motivation to continue using the app.
