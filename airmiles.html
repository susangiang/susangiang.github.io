AIR MILES Car & Hotel Booking

Redesigning the Car & Hotel booking experience

role

designer, internship, jan - apr 2016

I worked alongside a team consisting of another UX designer, a UX researcher, and software developers that implemented the proposed design recommendations. For this project, I got the opportunity to execute and assist with several UX research methods (heuristic evaluation, user testing, focus groups, and competitive analyses) and provided recommendations based on trends that were observed from the findings.

the problem

Issues with the AIR MILES Travel booking experiences (flight, car, and hotel) is one, if not the greatest, driver of calls to the call centre. With a limited number of Customer Care Specialists at the Care centre, wait times are high and the volume of calls are drastically larger during peak seasons for travel. Following a recent redesign of the flight booking experience on www.airmiles.ca, a redesign of the car and hotel booking experience was needed to maintain consistency across the platform and also address any usability issues that Collectors have encountered during their booking, resulting in a decline of successful car and hotel bookings.

research

digging a little deeper


Heuristic Evaluation

For this project, I first performed a heuristic evaluation of the existing car and hotel booking experience to identify any usability issues that I personally encountered. I found this evaluation valuable, as I was not a Collector of Miles prior to working for AIR MILES; therefore I was able to assess the workflow with a fresh pair of eyes and go through the experience as a Collector who is unfamiliar with the process.

User Testing

A total of seven Collectors were invited to the AIR MILES office to further identify common pain points that are potentially leading causes of unsuccessful bookings. These Collectors varied in age (late 20s to late 60s) and familiarity with technology (inexperienced with use of computers to expert users) and were a good representation of the wide range of Collectors that continue to collect Miles today.

The user testing was conducted to determine the following:

Comprehension:

  • On car and hotel booking details (search, location of car, map, insurance, fees)
  • On navigating the end-to-end booking experience

Ease of Use:

  • Ability to enter a valid search request
  • Ability to select a car/hotel nearby the arrival location
  • Ability to complete the booking process without guidance

Focus Groups

I, along with the UX Director and UX Researcher, drove down to the AIR MILES call centre (Care) to organize a focus group with five Customer Care Specialists. These specialists understand the needs of our Collectors very well, as they hear about the frustrations and concerns of our customers on a daily basis. Speaking with the specialists that deal directly with Collectors allowed us to gain valuable insight into potential issues that may not have been obvious to spot in other UX research methods.

Competitive Analysis

I had also conducted competitive analyses of our competitors and other existing car and hotel booking companies to identify the latest web usability trends in the travel industry.

Using the findings acquired from the heuristic evaluation, user testing, focus groups and competitive analyses, I was able to discover emerging themes and patterns that played an integral role in whether or not the Collector completes their booking successfully. Six major themes were observed: technical bugs, insufficient search results, usability/findability issues, booking flow issues, error handling issues, and overall comprehension.

the solution

During user testing, it was found that 7/7 users were not aware that there was a map feature that was included in the search results; we were able to verify that maps are a critical feature for selecting a car rental location as some users stated that they would not proceed with booking if they did not visually see the pick-up location relative to their entered destination. In the previous design, the map was collapsed and had a small hyperlink-blue link titled ‘View map’ on the far right side of a thin grey rectangle. It was evident that due to poor visibility, the users could not locate the map feature. To improve visibility, we designed the search results so that the map view is expanded by default, and the ‘View maps’ text was made considerably larger and bolder and was also accompanied by a location icon to the right of it to indicate that the map view can be toggled.

Not enough Miles to redeem a hotel

We also eliminated the use of vague error messaging in search results. Previously, when there are no car or hotels available based on the user’s entered criteria, a message simply telling the user to modify their search criteria and try again was presented to him/her. Playing on the error prevention design heuristic, the redesigned errors were made to be more informative and provided users with plausible explanations as to why an error occured, and how they can recover from it.
